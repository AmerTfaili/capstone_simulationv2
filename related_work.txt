2. Related Work
The literature on intelligent traffic signal control has expanded rapidly with the rise of deep reinforcement learning, graph-based coordination, and scalable multi-intersection control. This section reviews the existing literature across five key themes: (1) reinforcement-learning-based traffic signal control, (2) large-scale and network-level coordination, (3) graph-based and attention-based traffic signal learning, (4) critical-intersection selection under limited deployment, and (5) benchmarks, surveys, and recent evaluation trends. The review concludes with a synthesis of research gaps and how the present study addresses them.

2.1 Reinforcement-Learning-Based Traffic Signal Control
Reinforcement learning has become one of the most prominent approaches for adaptive traffic signal control because it enables a controller to learn signal timing strategies directly from traffic observations rather than relying only on fixed schedules or manually tuned rules. Early deep reinforcement learning studies showed that value-based methods could improve average travel time, delay, and queue performance by learning from queue length, waiting time, phase state, and lane occupancy information (Wei et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2019). As the field matured, attention shifted from simply proving that reinforcement learning could work to improving the quality of state, action, and reward design. Pressure-based learning became especially influential because it aligned the learning objective with network efficiency by rewarding phase choices that reduced pressure imbalances across incoming and outgoing links, while phase-competition modeling emphasized the structural conflict between movements and demonstrated that embedding traffic-signal phase relationships into the learning architecture could improve control performance and sample efficiency (Wei et al., 2019a; Zheng et al., 2019). Recurrent methods also gained interest because traffic control is inherently temporal, and historical context can be useful in partially observable or noisy traffic environments (Xu et al., 2020). More recent work has continued to refine deep reinforcement learning for traffic signal control through improved sampling, more robust reward definitions, and better adaptation to varying traffic regimes (Cai & Wei, 2024). Taken together, these studies show that reinforcement learning is a strong foundation for adaptive traffic control. However, much of this line of work concentrates on how to learn better signal policies once the control locations are already determined, with comparatively less attention paid to the deployment question of which intersections should receive adaptive intelligent control when full-network deployment is infeasible.

2.2 Large-Scale and Network-Level Coordination
As research moved from isolated intersections to urban road networks, scalability and coordination became central concerns. In multi-intersection environments, signalized intersections are strongly coupled through upstream-downstream flow interactions, queue spillback, and route competition, which means that a policy optimized only for local conditions can produce poor network-level outcomes, particularly in dense or highly interconnected traffic systems. Decentralized and multi-agent learning therefore emerged as natural extensions of single-intersection reinforcement learning, enabling multiple intersections to learn coordinated control strategies while interacting through the shared traffic environment (Chen et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020). Large-scale studies demonstrated that decentralized deep reinforcement learning can remain effective even when the number of intersections becomes substantial, provided that the controllers are given sufficient mechanisms for communication or indirect coordination (Chen et al., 2020). Transfer-oriented and meta-learning approaches further suggested that traffic signal controllers can reuse knowledge across scenarios rather than training a completely independent model for every new network configuration (Zang et al., 2020). More recently, scalable learning architectures such as federated reinforcement learning have been explored to reduce the overhead associated with training and coordinating multiple traffic agents, making network-level adaptive control more realistic for broader deployment (Bao et al., 2023). Despite these advances, a common assumption in many large-scale studies is that adaptive controllers are broadly available across the network. In practice, sensing, communication, and maintenance constraints often require only a subset of intersections to be upgraded with AI-based control, leaving an important gap between large-scale reinforcement learning and resource-aware deployment.

2.3 Graph-Based and Attention-Based Traffic Signal Learning
Traffic networks are relational systems, which makes graph-based learning especially well suited to traffic signal control. Graph attention and graph neural network methods have been introduced to capture spatial dependencies that are not well represented by purely local features or simple neighborhood averages. Network-level cooperation through graph attention showed that neighboring intersections do not contribute equally to traffic performance, and that learning attention weights over neighboring nodes can improve coordination quality (Wei et al., 2019b). More recent methods have extended this direction using graph neural networks, latent graph models, and graph-enhanced actor-critic formulations. Graph-neural-network-based traffic signal control has been used to learn both local and global network features, supporting adaptive decision making in single-intersection and multi-intersection settings, while latent graph reinforcement learning has been explored to provide more interpretable and semantically structured traffic representations, with the goal of improving robustness and decision quality in multi-intersection control (Jia et al., 2024; Sattarzadeh & Pathirana, 2024). Additional recent work has incorporated graph attention into multi-agent traffic signal control, reinforcing the view that spatial dependencies should be modeled explicitly rather than approximated implicitly (Yang et al., 2025). These graph-based studies clearly show that neighboring intersections should not be treated as identical. However, the literature has focused more heavily on using graph attention for message passing, cooperation, or state encoding than for directly shaping the reinforcement learning reward itself, leaving space for methods that use attention to shape the learning signal according to which neighbors matter most for congestion propagation.

2.4 Critical-Intersection Selection Under Limited Deployment
A separate but closely related line of research focuses on identifying important or critical intersections before applying intelligent control. This perspective is especially relevant when infrastructure or operational resources limit the number of intersections that can be upgraded with adaptive control. Critical-node-based traffic signal control demonstrated that prioritizing important intersections can improve network-wide performance even when only a subset of nodes is controlled adaptively (Xu et al., 2020). In this setting, node importance was compared against heuristic selection baselines such as traffic flow, betweenness centrality, and random selection, showing that the choice of controlled nodes can materially affect final traffic efficiency. The importance of this line of work is that it reframes signal control as both a learning problem and a deployment problem. Instead of assuming full-network adaptive control, it asks where limited control resources should be allocated for maximum system-level benefit. This is highly relevant in real deployments, where it is often not feasible to equip every signalized intersection with advanced sensing, communication, and learning infrastructure. At the same time, most critical-node-based methods still rely on rankings that are determined before training or remain relatively static throughout evaluation. Even when multiple criteria are considered, the selected set often does not evolve with the traffic conditions observed during the learning process. This limitation becomes more significant as the network grows or the traffic pattern shifts over time, leaving open the question of whether critical intersections should be re-evaluated dynamically during learning rather than selected once at the beginning of the control process.

2.5 Benchmarks, Surveys, and Evaluation Trends
The rapid growth of traffic signal control research has also been supported by benchmark environments, standardized simulators, and survey papers that organize the field. Large-scale simulation frameworks have enabled more consistent comparison across methods, with CityFlow becoming one of the most influential benchmark environments for studying reinforcement-learning-based traffic signal control at scale (Zhang et al., 2019). More recently, benchmark libraries have helped standardize evaluation protocols and facilitate reproducible comparisons across controllers, state designs, and traffic scenarios (Mei et al., 2024). These resources have been especially valuable because they reduce implementation-specific variability and make it easier to evaluate whether a new method offers meaningful performance gains. Survey papers have also documented several recurring open issues. Broad reviews of deep reinforcement learning in intelligent transportation systems identified scalability, realism, stability, and coordination as persistent challenges, while traffic-signal-specific reviews further emphasized the importance of reward design, partial observability, benchmark consistency, and cross-scenario generalization (Haydari & Yilmaz, 2020; Rasheed et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2021). More recent surveys have continued this discussion while highlighting coordination, graph-based learning, and deployment realism as active research directions in modern traffic signal control (Saadi et al., 2025; Xiao et al., 2026). Recent application-focused studies reinforce this conclusion: enhanced deep reinforcement learning methods continue to improve convergence and control robustness, while graph-neural and latent-graph approaches aim to improve interpretability and large-scale coordination (Cai & Wei, 2024; Jia et al., 2024; Sattarzadeh & Pathirana, 2024). However, the combined problem of dynamic critical-node selection, shared learning across a budgeted subset of nodes, and attention-aware reward shaping remains insufficiently explored in a unified framework.

2.6 Summary of Research Gaps and Study Contributions
Although the field of intelligent traffic signal control has made significant progress, several critical gaps remain that limit the practical deployment of graph-aware deep reinforcement learning in multi-intersection road networks. Many existing studies still rely on fixed critical-node rankings, assume that adaptive control can be deployed broadly across the network, or model neighboring intersections without clearly distinguishing their relative influence on congestion propagation. This study was explicitly designed to address these limitations and advance the field toward a more scalable, deployment-aware, and graph-sensitive control framework. More specifically, the following summarize the main gaps identified in the literature along with discussions on how this research work attempts to fill them:

- Gap 1 - Static critical-node selection under limited deployment: A major limitation in current critical-node traffic control literature is that node importance is often determined once using static or precomputed indicators such as flow, topology, or centrality, even though real traffic influence changes with evolving congestion patterns and control response (Xu et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2021).
  o How We Addressed It: This study introduces a phase-based critical-intersection reselection process that combines static graph information with dynamic traffic and control-related signals. By updating the selected set during training, the framework treats node selection as an adaptive process rather than a one-time preprocessing step.

- Gap 2 - Full-Network vs. Budgeted AI Deployment: Much of the reinforcement learning literature assumes that adaptive control can be applied widely across the road network, which is difficult to realize in practice due to sensing, communication, and infrastructure constraints (Chen et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2026).
  o How We Addressed It: Our framework explicitly assumes a limited control budget and selects only a subset of intersections for AI-based control. This makes the methodology more deployment-aware and better aligned with realistic traffic management scenarios.

- Gap 3 - Independent Multi-Agent Control vs. Shared Scalable Learning: Many traffic signal control methods either assign a separate controller to each node or rely on decentralized multi-agent coordination that becomes harder to scale as the number of intersections increases (Chen et al., 2020; Saadi et al., 2025).
  o How We Addressed It: We learn a shared deep Q-network across selected intersections, allowing multiple controlled nodes to benefit from a common policy. This reduces model complexity while preserving adaptive decision-making across the deployed control set.

- Gap 4 - Uniform vs. Unequal Neighbor Influence: Even when neighboring intersections are considered, many methods do not clearly distinguish how much each neighbor should influence learning, making it difficult to capture downstream congestion and spillback effects effectively (Wei et al., 2019b; Yang et al., 2025).
  o How We Addressed It: We incorporate attention-aware reward shaping so that neighboring intersections do not contribute equally by assumption. Instead, their influence on the reward is weighted according to learned relevance, providing a more robust signal for network-aware policy learning.

By addressing these core limitations, this study contributes a graph-aware, scalable, and deployment-conscious traffic signal control framework. It combines dynamic critical-intersection selection, shared reinforcement learning, and attention-aware neighbor influence within a single methodology for multi-intersection control under realistic resource constraints.

The core contributions of this research are:
1. A dynamic critical-intersection selection mechanism that combines static graph characteristics and dynamic traffic/control factors to update the selected node set over training phases.
2. A scalable framework for budgeted AI deployment in traffic signal control that learns a shared reinforcement learning model across selected intersections instead of requiring a separate policy for each node.
3. An attention-aware reward shaping strategy that uses learned neighbor importance to model unequal inter-intersection influence during reinforcement learning.
